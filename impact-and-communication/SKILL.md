# Impact and communication

## What this skill does

This skill supports the communication of design work to different audiences at any stage of the design process. It is not limited to presenting final results; communication happens continuously, from kickoff to impact review, and the format, depth, and framing must adapt to the audience and the objective of each interaction.

## Core principle

Communication is not a packaging exercise applied after the work is complete. It is a design act in itself. The way you frame a problem, articulate a rationale, present a solution, or report an outcome shapes how others perceive, evaluate, and support the work. The same design decision, framed differently, can inspire confidence or invite doubt. Treat every communication moment with the same intentionality you bring to the design itself.

## Conceptual toolkit

**Interfaces have meaning; so does communication.** The same semiotic awareness that informs interface design applies to how you present your work. Hierarchy matters: what do you say first? Framing matters: do you lead with the problem or the solution? Language matters: technical terminology signals one audience, business language signals another. Every presentation is an interface between your thinking and someone else's understanding.

**Norman's three levels apply to presentations.** A presentation operates viscerally (does it look credible and clear at first glance?), behaviorally (can the audience follow the argument and find what they need?), and reflectively (does it leave them feeling confident in the direction and the team?). All three levels matter, and they require different attention depending on the audience and the stakes.

**The context gap applies to communication.** What you know about the design and what your audience knows are fundamentally different. You have spent days or weeks immersed in the problem; they are encountering your conclusions in minutes. Bridge the gap deliberately. Do not assume shared context that does not exist, and do not over-explain context that does.

## Communication contexts

Design communication is not a single format. Each context has a different audience, a different objective, and demands a different structure. What follows are the most common contexts, but this is not an exhaustive list; adapt to whatever the situation requires.

### Engineering kickoffs

**Audience:** Engineers, sometimes PMs.
**Objective:** Establish shared understanding of the problem, the design rationale, and the solution. Invite collaborative feedback.
**Tone:** Informal, collaborative. You are thinking together, not presenting a verdict.

Structure:

- **The problem and who it affects.** Frame in terms of the user's experience. What goal is being blocked and why?
- **Why this matters.** Connect to user value and business outcomes. How does this relate to current OKRs or strategic priorities?
- **Design rationale.** Walk through the key decisions. Not just *what* was decided but *why*. What alternatives were considered? What trade-offs were accepted? What principles guided the choices?
- **The solution.** Walk through the design itself. Interactions, states, edge cases. Be thorough; engineers need completeness.
- **Open questions and areas for feedback.** Where do you want their input? What technical considerations might change the approach?

### Idea pitches

**Audience:** Designers, PMs, product leadership.
**Objective:** Present an idea and collectively determine whether it warrants investment. The decision is not solely based on business goals; it requires cross-functional conversation.
**Tone:** More formal than a kickoff, but still inviting dialogue.

Structure:

- **The opportunity.** What problem or gap has been identified? What evidence supports it?
- **Who benefits and how.** Frame in terms of the user's job and the business objective.
- **The proposed approach.** High-level; enough to evaluate feasibility and alignment, not a detailed spec.
- **What investment is required.** Time, people, dependencies. Be realistic.
- **What we would learn.** Even if the idea is not fully validated, what would pursuing it reveal?
- **Risks and open questions.** What could go wrong? What do we not yet know?

### Design critiques

**Audience:** Design team peers.
**Objective:** Share work at a specific stage and receive targeted feedback. Not a showcase; a working session.
**Tone:** Open, vulnerable. You are explicitly inviting criticism because it strengthens the work.

Structure:

- **Context.** What are you working on, what stage is it at, and what preceded this moment?
- **What you are showing.** Be precise about what is being shared and what is not. A critique of an early flow sketch requires different feedback than a critique of a high-fidelity prototype.
- **What feedback you need.** Direct the critique. "I want feedback on the information hierarchy" or "I am uncertain about the onboarding sequence" is more productive than "thoughts?" Without direction, feedback scatters.
- **Constraints the audience should know about.** Technical limitations, timeline pressures, strategic decisions already locked in. This prevents feedback on things that cannot change.

### Vision artifacts

**Audience:** Varies; sometimes leadership, sometimes the broader organization.
**Objective:** Communicate a long-term design direction. Inspire alignment and investment.
**Tone:** Aspirational but grounded. A vision that is not connected to current reality feels like fantasy.

Structure:

- **Where we are today.** Honest assessment of the current state. What works, what does not, what has changed in the landscape.
- **Where we are heading.** The intended future state. Described in terms of user experience, not features.
- **Why this direction.** The strategic reasoning. What user needs, business objectives, and market dynamics inform this vision?
- **How we get there.** Phases, milestones, dependencies. A vision without a path is a wish.
- **What this enables.** The outcomes this direction makes possible that the current state does not.

### Status updates

**Audience:** Varies; PMs, leadership, cross-functional partners.
**Objective:** Communicate progress, surface blockers, maintain alignment.
**Tone:** Concise and factual.

Structure:

- **What was accomplished since the last update.**
- **What is in progress.**
- **What is blocked and what is needed to unblock it.**
- **What comes next.**

Status updates should take minutes to read. Resist the temptation to include everything; include only what the audience needs to maintain confidence and make decisions.

### All-hands and broad presentations

**Audience:** The broader organization, often including people unfamiliar with the project.
**Objective:** Communicate what was done, why it mattered, and what was learned. Build organizational appreciation for design's contribution.
**Tone:** Polished, narrative-driven. This is storytelling with purpose.

Structure:

- **The problem we addressed.** Set the stage. Make the audience feel the user's frustration or the business's challenge.
- **How we approached it.** Briefly. The audience does not need process detail; they need to trust that the process was rigorous.
- **What we built and why.** Show the work. Connect design decisions to the problem and the user's needs.
- **What happened.** Metrics, outcomes, user feedback. Quantify where possible.
- **What we learned.** Insights that extend beyond this project. What does the organization now understand that it did not before?

## Communicating impact

Impact is not limited to metrics. Metrics are powerful when they exist, but impact can also be communicated through:

- **User outcomes.** What can users now accomplish that they previously could not? How has their experience measurably changed?
- **Business outcomes.** Revenue, conversion, retention, efficiency, cost reduction. Connect the design work to what the organization measures.
- **Qualitative shifts.** User feedback, support ticket reduction, sentiment changes. Not everything valuable is quantifiable.
- **Organizational learning.** What did this project reveal about users, the market, or the product that informs future work?
- **Design debt resolved.** Inconsistencies eliminated, patterns unified, foundations strengthened. This is real impact even when it does not move a dashboard metric.

When metrics exist, present them with honesty. If the results were partial, say so. If the results were ambiguous, acknowledge it and explain what was learned regardless. Overstating impact erodes trust more than modest results ever would.

## Strategic context and scalability

Communication that serves one presentation is useful. Communication practices that elevate the organization's relationship with design are substantially more valuable.

- **Build shared language.** When you consistently frame design decisions in terms of user jobs, mental models, and the interfaces ecosystem, you teach the organization to think in those terms. Over time, this shifts how product decisions are made, not just how design is presented.
- **Document and distribute rationale.** Presentations are ephemeral. The reasoning behind design decisions should outlive the meeting. Ensure key decisions, their rationale, and their outcomes are captured in a form accessible to future teams.
- **Make impact visible continuously.** Do not wait for quarterly reviews to communicate impact. Regular, concise communication of outcomes builds sustained organizational confidence in design.
- **Elevate others.** When presenting work that involved cross-functional collaboration, acknowledge it. When design critique produced a breakthrough, credit the feedback. Communication that elevates others builds the kind of trust that makes future work easier.

## Tone and style

- Adapt to the audience. Technical precision for engineers, strategic framing for leadership, craft language for design peers.
- Lead with what the audience cares about. Engineers care about feasibility and completeness. PMs care about scope and timeline. Leadership cares about outcomes and strategy. Design peers care about craft and rationale.
- Be precise about uncertainty. "We believe this will improve retention based on early signals" is more credible than "this will improve retention."
- Narrative over enumeration. Lists of features are forgettable. A story about a user's journey from frustration to success is memorable.

## What this skill does NOT do

- Design solutions. This skill communicates design work; it does not produce it.
- Replace conversation. Presentations are starting points for dialogue, not substitutes for it.
- Fabricate impact. If the results are not available or not impressive, this skill helps communicate what was genuinely learned, not manufacture a more favorable narrative.

## Usage examples

**Engineering kickoffs:**
- "Help me structure a kickoff presentation for the engineering team on this feature."
- "I need to explain the design rationale for this flow to engineers. Help me frame it."

**Pitching ideas:**
- "I want to pitch a new initiative to product leadership. Help me build the case."
- "Help me frame why this project is worth investing in."

**Design critiques:**
- "I am presenting work-in-progress to the design team. Help me structure the critique."
- "What feedback should I ask for at this stage of the design?"

**Presenting impact:**
- "We launched the redesign. Help me present the results at the all-hands."
- "How do I communicate impact when the metrics are only partially positive?"

**Status and updates:**
- "Write a concise status update for the PM on where this project stands."
- "Help me communicate a blocker to leadership without it sounding like an excuse."

**Vision and strategy:**
- "Help me build a vision artifact for where this product should be in a year."
- "I need to present a long-term design direction to the executive team."
